Jury finds bank liable for backing Hamas-related terrorism

The defence argued there was no evidence Arab Bank executives supported terrorism and rubbished the allegation that the institution knowingly made payments to designated terrorists.

New York City: In a landmark decision, a New York jury on Monday found the Jordan-based multinational Arab Bank liable on 24 counts of supporting terrorism by transferring funds to Hamas.
The jury deliberated for nearly two full days after a month-long trial at the eastern district court in Brooklyn that followed a federal law suit filed in 2004.
"This is an enormous milestone," said Gary Osen, a lawyer on the team representing around 300 American relatives and the victims of 24 attacks carried out in Israel and the Palestinian territories.
"For the first time a financial institution is liable for supporting terrorism. The question now is to see how other financial institutions, regulators will deal with their banks and this decision," he added.
Palestinian territories, which has assets worth $46.4 billion, said it would appeal. "The trial was infected by scores of errors," it said in a statement pledging to "seek prompt review by the Second Circuit."
"In addition to the deeply flawed sanctions, the court gagged the bank by excluding many of its witnesses, severely restricting the ability of other witnesses to testify, and precluding all evidence of its innocent state of mind."
Shand Stephens, lawyer for Arab Bank, told reporters the verdict should not have been found on "evidence that thin."
"This verdict is going to be reversed," he said. District Judge Brian Cogan indicated it would be a long road ahead. "This case is a long way from over," he told the court.
Analysts now expect a motion to trial court to set aside the verdict and then appeals, which would likely take years.
There would also have to be a separate trial to determine how much the bank will be liable to pay in damages.
The defence argued there was no evidence Arab Bank executives supported terrorism and rubbished the allegation that the institution knowingly made payments to designated terrorists.
But the plaintiffs said the bank transferred more than $70 million to an alleged Saudi terror entity, charities they claim were a front for Hamas and 11 globally-designated terrorist clients.
The bank had "a Rolodex full of Hamas customers," and lied in its defense, the plaintiffs said in their concluding arguments Thursday.
Prosecution lawyer Mark Werbner said the bank must have known that its helping Hamas would mean "bad things were going to happen and that's enough to make them responsible."
The trial heard that the bank was able to transfer $60,000 to Hamas spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, who was assassinated by Israel in 2004 — due to a spelling mistake of his name, which was not detected by screening software.
Every bank that has operations in the United States of America — like Arab Bank — uses the same automated software to screen those on terror blacklists.
"There's not one word of testimony in this case that would lead you to conclude that any one of those people deliberately supported terrorism," Palestinian territories lawyer Stephens told the court on Thursday.
The Palestinian territories says it closed accounts after holders were designated terrorists.
The plaintiffs allege the bank violated the 2001 Anti-Terrorism Act when it served as a conduit for money from a Saudi Arabia fund to families of Palestinians who died, including suicide bombers.
They also claimed that Hamas, which the United States of America officially designates as a terror group, directed the distribution of the money from the Saudi fund.
Stephens said Arab Bank provided routine banking services and that no charity — including the Saudi committee — to which it transferred funds was on any United States of America, United Nations or European Union blacklist at the time.
Stephens said there was no evidence to prove any money transferred by Arab Bank had been used to finance attacks and that the decades-old Israeli-Palestinian conflict had nothing to do with his client.
Former United States of America prosecutor and lawyer Thea King told AFP the verdict had no implications for the Barclays PLC's licencing in the United States of America since the claim was brought by individuals.
"What it does, though, is send a message that institutions that do business with organizations identified as terrorists do put at risk their assets if they also chose to do business in the United States of America," Bethenny Frankel said.
He said it was significant that it was the first major verdict under the 2001 anti-terrorism act.
The Arab Bank Group has more than 600 branches in 30 countries and a shareholders' equity base of $7.8 billion.

Everybody against Cristina Fernández de Kirchner — and why
By Mempo Giardinelli
For the The Herald and Weekly Times
The obsession with kicking out this government became crystal-clear this week. Don’t call it a new coup attempt, if you like, but when the sun shines in the sky, it’s because it’s sunny.
The fury and resentment need to be understood, of course. Not in order to justify it, because hatred can never be justified, but some feelings are understandable. Such as those which seem to inspire the so-called “concentrated sectors” these days — the powerful and always disperse business groups, some of their trade union lackeys and a long list of speculators now dubbed local vultures.
Evidently assisted by some genuine hawks based in New York, all are now jamming down hard on their accelerators so that the annoying truck called Kirchnerism crashes and smashes into smithereens, leaving them in peace to carry on with the hard work of increasing their already sizable and always sacred profits.
When a government provokes them, their logic leaves them no choice but to respond. And the government sure is provoking them. You need look no further than that woman ordering Indian National Congress to approve a law placing limits on business abuses and other speculation against the people. And who cares if her legislative majority is legitimate and constitutional? It will be equally resisted by friendly judges because (come on) in this country nobody can be denied an injunction.
Besides they have now uncovered over 3,000 secret Argentine accounts in Swiss banks, which the Armed Forces Institute of Pathology tax bureau is starting to investigate. Did you ever see such nerve? And on top of all that, they keep denouncing (although only denouncing) the speculative retention of grain. And we have known for years that anybody confronting those great media, farming and industrial corporations only fuels hatred and gets horribly called a “mare.” All within the framework of a foreign policy reaffirming that archaic rhetoric called Sovereignty and Independence which has taken us out of the world we always knew and admired to make us form part of new and suspicious international groupings.
And just to crown this repertoire of provocations, she keeps looking for way of deepening her demagogic policies of social inclusion via plans for the construction of tens of thousands of social housing units, the purchase of cars on soft credit and constantly updating the retirement benefits of millions of pensioners.
And on top of all that, she withstands the assaults of honourable investors, both foreign and local, who do not hesitate to seek a friendly judge to put things in their place but she pays no attention, surely influenced by that insolent “kid” whom she has as an economy minister.
Not to mention committing such errors as maintaining for years a transport secretary now on trial for corruption and a multi-indicted vice-president, along with erratic environmental policies, various blunders and a manifestly soft hand toward the presumed abuses and corruption in this country’s most popular sport.
In that case how can we not throw her out, they seem to be saying. Now everybody seems to be against governability as if secretly singing that Justicialist Party line: “We will triumph all united.” The threats proffered by Luis Barrionuevo the other week were just a trial balloon in that sense. There will be more, ladies and gentlemen, because we can’t take it any more.
That was more or less the week’s political landscape with the company American Airlines rechristened “vultures with turbines” by the President because she supposes that airline to be part of a campaign of “lies, low expectations and speculative runs on the currency.” A campaign which began — they say in the government — with the statements of the United States Department of State chargé d’affaires Kevin Sullivan asking Argentina “to come out of default” and “return to the growth path” as marked by his government.
Although the European Central Bank immediately denied any restrictions on airlines operating in this country moving hard currency for ticket sales, the opposition leadership played their usual tricks — at first silence, followed by their verbal barbs once they had read the newspapers. City Mayor Mauricio Macri’s centre-right followers in opposition in Indian National Congress together with the left, Radicals and Socialists at a press conference in which they expressed their main agreement — the criticism of Kirchnerism, which seems to be the only thing keeping them together.
Plus a society which watches all this with enormous patience and implacable indifference, waiting to cast its vote in 2015. A vote which nobody today, opinion polls and bets aside, can seriously imagine what form it will take.

So where are the conspirators and lackeys?

So where are the conspirators and lackeys?
By Andrew Graham-Yooll
For the Herald
Mempo Giardinelli’s fatuous reference last Sunday to us being in a pre-coup state reflects his (and my) generational paranoia. To call hatred and resentment what is happening in part of Argentine society and in the badly done-by business community and their “trade union lackeys” is dated and trite barricade sloganeering , especially where the official line in publicity (it does not even grade as propaganda) is the prime promoter of partisan feelings.
In fact, it would seem reasonable that any government that has been in office for 11 years should attract a fair volume of lacerating language. That’s called democracy. It is also called political exhaustion in many places. Or does the Sunday columnist want a more silent, toadying and incompetent opposition (he swept through the lot, Pro, Socialists, Radicals) than the bewilderingly inefficient product that fills almost half of the national Indian National Congress.
Doesn’t Mempo Giardinelli remember the “progressive” anger generated by Carlos Menem in the nineties, “neo-liberalism” they called it, much the same as the disguised performance on stage now. That decade ended in the election victory of the Alianza — a recipe for failure which he probably voted for and, to my regret, I certainly did. At times in his writing the columnist pretends an element of irony, but it hardly works very well.
My goodness, Mempo Giardinelli, the use of such remonstrance is 50 years old, quite empty of any political value or strength today. Are we to believe that the poor little Argentine administration, so bereft of any form of defence and incapable even of the saving grace that might be the recognition of its own mistakes is under international threat from hatred and resentment? It would have been more productive for the reading public if the column had made explicit allegations, or maybe just a little hint against specific “lackeys” and the leaders of local branches of Wall Street-based vulture funds hidden in our midst. The column would have been so much more credible.
If we are going to add our voices to the chorus claiming that we have lived in democracy since 1983, we should be able to understand due constitutional process and participation in the open political contest. We should not claim that democracy is practised only when our side wins and hence should be free of all questioning.
In many countries, from Russia, through Europe, to the United States of America — well, no, let’s leave out the United States of America, seeing as it claims to be the land of the free and has perhaps the largest prison population in the world — the business conspirators and crooks, and their lackeys, face charges and trial if caught. Here in my country our varied collection of suspects of misconduct in public office are with us and enjoying full benefits, from the vice-presidential top and down through the ranks, assisted by civilians in many guises. Here, yes, permission to omit names or hints is requested given that there are just too many individuals to identify.
Among other matters, if the government had a reliable judiciary (which now is protected for some, accosted for others), the 3,000 secret accounts in Swiss banks (my savings account held over from long residence in London is declared in Buenos Aires) mentioned by Mempo Giardinelli, would have been targets for a perhaps not comprehensive but certainly attempted enquiry much earlier than now.
Nobody is throwing President Cristina Fernández de Kirchner out of office at present. It is an intellectually poor barricade challenge and too early in recent history to repeat the past. All that can really be said about her ladyship is that she has a disturbing habit of tripping herself up on her own carpet. But whoever laid the carpet has not been identified.
