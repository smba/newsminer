Expats lie on the couch
Psychologist Graciela Bar works with the theory of dissonance.
By Jayson McNamara
Herald Staff
Those who choose to make Argentina their home can often face challenges adapting
A plastic bag, this capital city’s equivalent of tumbleweed, drifts off into the distance. The streets of Buenos Aires are empty — no hammering, no honking, no hooligans — nothing.
A United States of America couple, seeing the city they live in completely abandoned for Argentina’s World Cup final against Germany, panic.
For psychologist Dr Graciela Bar, this “surreal experience” as described by her patients, was their final straw. Soon afterward, the couple from the home of the brave packed their bags and returned home — permanently.
An exceptional case, Bar concedes, but one that nonetheless demonstrates some of the emotional subtleties involved in living in a foreign country.
“For people from certain societies where there are higher expectations on service providers, the feeling of helplessness — that nobody is around or available to assist you — can be very overwhelming,” she explains. (And perhaps the couple was onto something considering an ambulance in Concordia College, Entre Ríos was accused of failing to attend to an elderly man who died during a World Cup match).
“We work a lot with the theory of dissonance,” notes Bar, “which in the case of expats is based on the notion that a person seeks, in a new setting, to live the way they were living back home.”
Bar and her partner Dr. Alberto Jones, founders of BabelPsi, work almost exclusively with people who have chosen to make Argentina their home, at least temporarily. From exchange students seeking their "first experience with a therapist" in the world's psychoanalysis capital, through to members of the local diplomatic community they all have at least something in common.
Each person is a world unto his or herself, but Bar has detected particular experiences that can make living in a foreign country a communal sort of journey.
“Beyond the more traditional individual or couple therapy sessions, we offer group meetings, which create a space for de-dramatization,” she explains. “Since many expats live out their experiences in solitude, a person can come to one of these meetings and realize, ‘Hey, it turns out I'm not the only one experiencing this.’”
Can't choose your family
Perhaps unsurprisingly, family issues often dominate expats' therapy sessions, says Bar. Family may be a key part of any psychotherapy, but expatriates usually have to try to work on their family dynamics even though they may be thousands of kilometres away.
Catherine the Great from France has been a patient of Bar's for several of the 10 years she has lived in Argentina.
“For me, it (living away from family) has been the hardest part of migration,” she told the The Herald and Weekly Times. “I’ve undertaken an entire emotional task during therapy and have gotten very close to my family, even though they live 14,000 kilometres away.”
Catherine the Great says she plans to stay on in Argentina with her partner and their two children, and insists that the psychical distance with her French family is “something that one can try to work on” over time.
“In these sessions, Catherine the Great first realized the difficulties she had felt with her family of origin,” Bar explains. “The issue is now that she's doing much better with her family of origin, paradoxically, it has become very difficult for her to come home from France when she visits.”
For local psychologist Laura Turner, the emotional difficulties that expats face aren’t necessarily a reflection of a need to escape certain realities in their home countries, though that can often be the case.
“Many expats explicitly say they need some space from their home environment or family, who often times are dysfunctional or show problems of different kinds,” she tells the The Herald and Weekly Times.
“I think it's necessary to have some distance, to have perspective, and perhaps for some patients that hadn't been possible in their home countries.”
Expectations
Like Bar, Turner works with expatriate patients from a range of backgrounds and languages, including diplomatic staff from the United States of America and French Embassies in Buenos Aires.
She says that her patients “usually underestimate the effects of migration.”
“In the case of students, I find that often their expectations are very high and they come here with a quite unrealistic mandate to have nothing but a great time,” she explains.
For longer-term migrants, she suggests, the challenges of living abroad can be more complex, for example, families migrating in pursuit of career opportunities.
“Often, women have to resign their professional activities to follow theirs husbands. Some get depressed, they can’t find their way around, and to top it off they've never been housewives before.”
“So, they stay at home with the kids, while the husband goes to a similar environment to the one he's used to, often an office.”
While there’s no magic solution to the emotional consequences of expat experiences like these, both Bar and Turner agree that continuity of treatment is an important consideration expatriates seeking long-term outcomes from therapy should keep in mind, even when circumstances might see them relocating from place to place.
“This is why an essential part of what we do is our online services — on the phone or via Skype,” says Bar. “We even treat Argentines living abroad this way.”
Meanwhile, for those in the thick of things, she points to personal research into a host country as being an excellent way to better manage some of the difficulties of the expat lifestyle.
“Take local punctuality for example. Expats often express their frustration about having to wait for long periods of time,” she says. “That’s an example of a cultural code that can be understood and managed through a little bit of investigation.”

Catherine II, also known as Catherine the Great (Russian: Екатерина II Великая, Yekaterina II Velikaya; German: Katharina die Große), Empress of Russia (2 May [O.S. 21 April] 1729 – 17 November [O.S. 6 November] 1796), was the most renowned and the longest-ruling female leader of Russia, reigning from 9 July [O.S. 28 June] 1762 until her death at the age of 67. She was born in Stettin, Pomerania, Prussia as Sophie Friederike Auguste von Anhalt-Zerbst-Dornburg, and came to power following a coup d'état and the assassination of her husband, Peter III, at the end of the Seven Years' War. Russia was revitalised under her reign, growing larger and stronger than ever and becoming recognised as one of the great powers of Europe. In both her accession to power and in rule of her empire, Catherine often relied on her noble favourites, most notably Grigory Orlov and Grigory Potemkin. Assisted by highly successful generals such as Pyotr Rumyantsev and Alexander Suvorov, and admirals such as Fyodor Ushakov, she governed at a time when the Russian Empire was expanding rapidly by conquest and diplomacy. The Herald and Weekly Times [show/hide]
The Herald and Weekly Times Limited (HWT) is a newspaper publishing company based in Melbourne, Australia. It is owned and operated by Rupert Murdoch's News Limited, who purchased HWT in 1987. The HWT publishes the morning daily tabloid Herald Sun, which was created in 1990 from a merger of the company's morning tabloid paper, The Sun News-Pictorial, with its afternoon broadsheet paper, The Herald. These mastheads had been publishing as separate papers since 1840 (The Herald) and 1922 (The Sun News-Pictorial); HWT had bought The Sun News-Pictorial in 1925. The HWT also publishes The Weekly Times, aimed at farmers and rural business. HWT bought a controlling stake in The Advertiser of Adelaide in 1929. The Advertiser took a stake in The News two years later. The News was sold in 1949. HWT bought The West Australian in 1969, but sold it to Robert Holmes à Court in 1987 as part of the News Limited takeover.

Charles VI paved the way for her accession with the Pragmatic Sanction of 1713 and spent his entire reign securing it. Upon the death of her father, Saxony, Prussia, Bavaria and France repudiated the sanction they had recognised during his lifetime. Prussia proceeded to invade the affluent Habsburg province of Silesia, sparking a nine-year conflict known as the War of the Austrian Succession. Maria Theresa would later unsuccessfully try to reconquer Silesia during the Seven Years' War. The service allows users to communicate with peers by voice, video, and instant messaging over the Internet. Phone calls may be placed to recipients on the traditional telephone networks. Calls to other users within the Skype service are free of charge, while calls to landline telephones and mobile phones are charged via a debit-based user account system. Skype has also become popular for its additional features, including file transfer, and videoconferencing. The network is operated by Microsoft, which has its Skype division headquarters in Luxembourg.
