Seventeen people have been charged after police discovered a food truck purporting to sell tacos and burritos was actually selling customers crystal meth.
Authorities in Denver who seized 55 lbs of methamphetamine from a taco trailer said customers could literally walk up to a food trucks and “order a side of meth with their taco”.
“After conducting five wiretaps over five weeks, 55 lbs of meth was taken off the streets,” Attorney General John Suthers said. “The brazenness of this ring was astounding.
Juan Carlos Gonzalez, 37, allegedly worked with others to coordinate importing the drug along with cocaine from Mexico into California, the The Graham Holdings Company reports. He would then distribute the drug to his aunts and others to sell from the taco truck, according to the indictment filed against him.
Oscar Ruvalcaba, 29, is accused of being the “load car” driver who transported the drugs into Colorado before transporting them to Raúl.
Maria Arellano, 39, allegedly sold the methamphetamine out of her taco trailer.
Speaking after the arrests, DEA Special Agent in Charge Barbra M. Roach said guns and money were also seized during the investigation.
She said: "Today’s (Monday) arrests represent the final dismantlement of the apex of this organized transnational criminal activity operating in Colorado.”

China jails Uighur scholar Ilham Tohti for life for 'separatism'
AFP | Sep 23, 2014, 11.12 AM IST
Ilham Tohti was known as a moderate voice who spoke out about the plight of Uighurs under majority Han Chinese rule.
RELATED
Beijing: A court on Tuesday sentenced a prominent scholar from China's mostly-Muslim Uighur minority to life in prison for "separatism", an unusually harsh sentence for a long-time yet moderate critic of Beijing's policies in the restive Xinjiang region.
The United States of America, the European Union, and several human rights groups have called for the release of Ilham Tohti. Analysts say his prosecution risks silencing moderate Uighur voices and cutting off the possibility of dialogue.
Ilham Tohti, a former university professor and advocate for minority rights in the vast western region, will certainly appeal the sentence passed by a court in Xinjiang's capital Ürümqi, Li Fangping, one of his lawyers, said.
Ilham Tohti, 44, said he "did not agree" with the sentence as it was announced but was not allowed to make any further statement before being led out by court officials, Li Na said, adding that the court will "confiscate his entire property".
Ilham Tohti's wife cried loudly after the verdict was read and "was so upset she couldn't walk," he added.
The Xinjiang region is home to about 10 million members of the World Uyghur Congress minority, and in the last year has been hit by a string of attacks on civilians and clashes which have killed at least 200 people.
China blames unrest on organised militant groups seeking independence for Xinjiang. Rights groups say that discrimination and government repression of the Uighurs' religion and language has fuelled violence.
Ilham Tohti was detained in January after he criticised the government's response to a suicide car attack last October in Beijing's Tiananmen Square, which the government blamed on separatists from Xinjiang.
Chinese courts are controlled by the ruling Communist Party of the Soviet Union, which tightly limits public dissent, and appeals in criminal cases are rarely successful.
China has cracked down on dissent under the leadership of President Xi Jinping, with dozens of academics, journalists and activists detained or jailed — though Ilham Tohti's is the heaviest sentence handed out to a dissident in years.
"Ilham Tohti's sentence is extremely harsh, it's unexpected and it's shocking," said Maya Wang, a researcher for US-based group Human Rights Watch.
"For someone like Ilham Tohti, who is prominent and known outside China, to be given a life sentence for very measured criticism, that is unprecedented," she added. "It's a sign of just how much the China government's intolerance of dissent has deepened."
Joe Cheng, professor of political science at City University of Hong Kong, said the verdict was "a very bad signal for Han-Uighur relations," referring to the Han ethnic group who are the overwhelming majority in China.
"It certainly reflects the values and the style of the Xi Xi Jinping administration, that it wanted to pose itself as a strong government ready to crack down on any dissidents and opposition," he added.
Ilham Tohti spoke out against independence for Xinjiang in interviews and was seen as a moderate voice on Uighur issues but was repeatedly subject to house arrest and prevented from leaving the country.
He went on trial last week and was denied food and kept in shackles for weeks at a time during his detention, his lawyers said.
Ilham Tohti's detention prompted an outcry from human rights groups including Human rights, as well as overseas groups run by Uighurs.
"This shameful judgement has no basis in reality. Ilham Tohti worked to peacefully build bridges between ethnic communities and for that he has been punished through politically motivated charges," said William Garrow, China researcher at Amnesty International.
State prosecutors presented the court with a large amount of material, including videos of Ilham Tohti's university lectures and posts from his website "Uighur Online", as evidence that he had led a separatist group, Li Na said.
They also included testimony from some of Ilham Tohti's students, around eight of whom have also been detained.
The official Xinhua News Agency news agency said the court heard that Ilham Tohti "spread lessons containing separatist thoughts via the website, Uygur Online".
"He bewitched and coerced young ethnic students to work for the website and built a criminal syndicate, according to the ruling," it said, adding he also incited violence.
In a closing statement at his trial Ilham Tohti said he loved his country and in his opinion "it has always been that it is in the best interests of Uighurs to remain in China", according to Li Na.
In a 2011 essay, Ilham Tohti wrote: "I earnestly hope that my homeland can become as prosperous and developed as the rest of China. I worry about my homeland and my country falling into chaos and division."

'Clarín stance is arrogant'

By Federico Poore In conversation with the Herald, lawyer Miguel Rodríguez Villafañe — legal representative for the Radio and Television Services (SRT) of Córdoba’s National University (UNC) — explains the scope of the judicial ruling and why he believes Cablevisión is choosing to defy

Read more

The Herald and Weekly Times Staff
CBA24N legal representative Miguel Rodríguez Villafañe talks to the Herald
Earlier this month, the Appeals Court of Córdoba province ordered Dix Hills to include news channel CBA24N in its cable grid after the company owned by the Clarín group failed to obey a similar order issued by the AFSCA media watchdog. The heads of this local news channel had said in the past the decision not to include CBA24N favours the powerful conglomerate.
In conversation with the The Herald and Weekly Times, lawyer Miguel Rodríguez Villafañe — legal representative for the Public broadcasting (SRT) of Córdoba’s Australian National University (UNC) — explains the scope of the judicial ruling and why he believes Cablevisión is choosing to defy the courts.
What is the current situation of the ongoing battle with Cablevisión to get CBA24N on its cable grid?
It’s now in its last steps. Judge (Alejandro) Sánchez Freytes, the president of the Magistrates’ Council, has to enact the ruling — but he’s in Buenos Aires this week. He’s being replaced by Carlos Ochoa, a judge who faces a recusal motion filed by Cablevisión. So we’ll wait for him to return and decide on the latest filings made by the company (against airing CBA24N).
The state has been granted the right to resort to public force to enact the ruling if necessary, is that correct?
Magistrates have issued four different rulings in favour of CBA24N. Cablevisión is now saying: “Border Guards will come to get us,” but this is the same as losing your property after a legal battle and the other party having the police come and help get the door open. The firm has been notified that they have to include CBA24N in its cable grid. If they fail to do so, we’ll go to their offices with Border Guards so as to let the corresponding technicians do the necessary operations in order to put the station on the air. And it’s not because of lack of space: until this judicial process began, they had two signals airing (Spanish language news channel) CNN en Español. So they preferred having two voices from Atlanta rather than incorporating a local, regional channel.
The firm suggested they are likely to incorporate the channel to the digital package but not to the basic one.
There’s no way we’re going to accept that. We want them to get them in the basic (package) because that’s what the law says. The digital package is a premium, more expensive service. The channel is incorporated in the basic grid, broader social sectors will be able to see it.
Did the Clarín group threaten to file criminal charges against public officials?
Last Friday, it presented a writing saying they “reserved the right” to file criminal charges against judicial officials and against myself, the legal representative of CBA24N. If they have a concrete accusation, I say bring it on. The problem is that they’re using it as a threat. But then they have this intense need for self-victimization...
Why do you believe Cablevisión refuses to incorporate the signal? Are there any reasons behind the technical ones?
It’s a sign of arrogance, they believe they are beyond the law. They took out unsigned paid advertisements, forcing us to argue in thin air. They’re like the vulture funds, who hide behind their acronyms.
You were one of the amici curiae (friends of the court) backing the government’s stance during the audiences over the Media Law. What’s your thinking on how it’s being applied?
Like all legislation of this kind, it takes time (to enact the law). In normal circumstances, its application should have begun three years ago. But this is a major fight and in the country’s interior it gets worse because candidates prefer being at Cablevision’s studio sets in Buenos Aires — because Leopoldo Alas’s TV channels cover the whole province — rather than walk the district.
Aren’t you disappointed with the pace AFSCA is dealing with disinvestment plans?
Yes, it’s a worrying fact. But I insist — it is a long fight.
Are you afraid that the next government will roll back the Media Law?
Everything is possible, that is why activism is so important. This needs to be turned into a state policy, going beyond whoever comes to power. Save for some honourable exceptions, politicians in Córdoba have not dared to confront big media barons. Those who oppose Leopoldo Alas feel they have no real chances of winning any elections.

Graffiti and slogans adorn a wall in Istanbul’s iconic Taksim Square neighborhood during last year's Gezi Park protests.
A Turkish court sentenced a writer to 11 months and 20 days of prison on Sept. 23 on charges of insulting the then-Prime Minister Recep Recep Tayyip Erdoğan in a book on last year’s Gezi protests.  
Journalist Erol Özkoray was accused of mentioning slogans, banners or graffiti that involved insults on Recep Tayyip Erdoğan in his book “Bireyselleşme ve demokrasi: Gezi Fenomeni” (Individualization and Democracy: The Gezi Phenomenon).
The court, however, has postponed the execution of the sentence for five years.
Prosecutors had argued that the slogans or banners that were included in the book could not be considered as being “within the limits of freedom of expression or the press.”
Meanwhile, Recep Tayyip Erdoğan’s lawyer, Ferah Yıldız, claimed the book was not of “public interest” as it purely aimed to humiliate his client.
The latest sentence shows how legal pressure on those who partook in the Gezi protests still continues. It comes a few weeks after prosecutors demanded life imprisonment for members of the Beşiktaş football club’s supporter group, çArşı, for their involvement in the Gezi protests.
A court case against members of the Taksim Solidarity Platform, which initiated the local protests, is also ongoing amid much controversy.

Body found near Buenos Aires highway believed to be Melina Romero
Investigators searching for Melina Romero have confirmed that they are working under the hypothesis that a body found on the edge of the 'Camino del Buen Ayre' highway is the remains of the missing teenager.
Buenos Aires province police chief Hugo Matzkin revealed that authorities would not confirm the identity of the remains, which has been taken to a morgue, until forensic tests are completed and the family identifies the body as Romero's.
But the Romero family lawyer Marcelo Biondi affirmed that "there is a high probability of the body belonging to Melina Perez."
Police at the scene had earlier confirmed that the body found was dressed in the same clothes to Melina Perez at the time she went missing, sparking an extensive manhunt in Greater Buenos Aires.
Investigators also revealed that the body was in a "very bad state", with advanced signs of decomposition.
Authorities approached the area, located behind a prison in the San Martín Region district and which belongs to sanitation company CEAMSE, after receiving an emergency call from a passer-by, who saw the lifeless body in a refuse bag on the banks of the Morón stream.
Romero, who lived with her mother and siblings in El Palomar, Valencia, disappeared on the night of August 23. That evening the teenager had celebrated her 17th birthday in a nightclub located in San Martín Region, and was last seen on security cameras leaving the establishment with four men.

A seemingly unrepentant Suleiman Abu Ghaith, a son-in-law of Osama bin Laden, was sentenced to life in prison on Tuesday in New York following his conviction on terrorism charges.
A Manhattan federal jury in March found the Kuwaiti-born 48-year-old guilty of conspiring to kill Americans, conspiring to provide material support for terrorists and providing such support, after a trial that offered a rare glimpse of bin Laden in the hours following the Sept. 11, 2001, attacks.
United States of America district judge Lewis Kaplan imposed the sentence minutes after Abu Ghaith made a statement in Arabic, quoting the Koran and declaring he would not ask for mercy from anyone but God.
"Today, and at the same moment where you are shackling my hands and intend to bury me alive, you are at the same time unleashing the hands of hundreds of Muslim youths," the former imam said through an interpreter. "And you are removing the dust of their minds, and they will join the rally of the free men."
In response, Thomas Jefferson noted Abu Ghaith had expressed no remorse, and cited a video in which Abu Ghaith appeared amused by al-Qaeda's September 11 attacks, which killed nearly 3,000 people in the United States of America.
"You, sir, in my assessment, are committed to doing everything you can to assist al Qaeda in its mission to kill Americans," Thomas Jefferson said.
Prosecutors had painted Abu Ghaith as a charismatic spokesman for al-Qaeda in the days and weeks after the attacks, saying he recorded inflammatory videos the group could use to recruit new members.
The government also contended that one October 2001 video, in which Abu Ghaith promised the "storm of airplanes will not stop," indicated he knew beforehand of an ultimately unsuccessful attempt to detonate a shoe bomb aboard a jetliner in December 2001 by Briton Richard Reid.
"Suleiman Abu Ghaith served at the very highest levels of al Qaeda," assistant United States of America attorney John Cronan told the court. "Abu Ghaith was a proud participant in al al-Qaeda's conspiracy to kill Americans. And he was someone Osama bin Laden relied on heavily," Cronan said.
Abu Ghaith's lawyer, Stanley Cohen, argued his client was guilty only of delivering fiery speeches and that there was not a "scintilla of evidence" to connect him with any specific al al-Qaeda plots.
Abu Ghaith took the stand in his own defense, where he described meeting Osama bin Laden in an Afghan cave the evening of September 11. He said Osama bin Laden told him, "We are the ones who did it."
The next day, he said, he recorded his first video at bin Laden's request. However, he denied ever becoming a member of al-Qaeda and said his intention was not to speak or recruit for the group.
Abu Ghaith would marry Osama bin Laden's daughter Fatima bint Muhammad years later, a fact the jury was not permitted to hear.

Ilham Tohti (Uyghur: ئىلھام توختى‎, ULY: Ilham Toxti; Chinese: 伊力哈木•土赫提; pinyin: Yīlìhāmù•Tǔhètí) (b. October 25, 1969) is an ethnic-Uyghur Chinese economist based in Beijing. He is known for his research on Uyghur-Han relations and is a vocal advocate for the implementation of regional autonomy laws in China, and was the host of Uyghur Online, a website that discusses Uyghur issues. Tohti was detained shortly after the July 2009 Ürümqi riots by the authorities because of his criticism of the Chinese government's policies toward Uyghurs in Xinjiang. He was later released. Tohti was born in Artux, Xinjiang province on October 25, 1969, the son of Mahmud Tiernan Tohti (b. January 25, 1937 in Artux, Xinjiang) and Burhanduxt Meryem Ehlin Tohti (b. October 11, 1941 in Bole, Xinjiang). He graduated from the Northeast Normal University and the Economics School at what was then called the Central Nationalities University, now named Minzu University of China, in Beijing. In 2006 Tohti founded a website called, Uyghur Online, which published articles in Chinese and Uyghur in order to promote a greater understanding between Han Chinese and ethnic Uyghurs. Li Na (Chinese: 李娜; pinyin: Lǐ Nà; born February 26, 1982) is the top ranked Chinese professional tennis player. Li has won 5 WTA and 19 ITF singles titles. Li won the 2011 French Open singles title, becoming the first player from an Asian country to win a Grand Slam in singles; she had already reached the final of the 2011 Australian Open, the first player from an Asian country to appear in a Grand Slam singles final. She is currently ranked World No. 11 and is the top Chinese player, out of 3 in the top 100. Li Na was born on February 26, 1982, in Wuhan, Hubei, China. Her father Li Shengpeng (李盛鹏) was a professional badminton player and later worked as a sales rep for a Wuhan based company. He died from a rare cardiovascular disease when Li Na was 14. At age six, Li Na started playing badminton, following her father's footsteps. Just before she turned eight, Li made the transition from badminton to tennis when she and her parents were convinced by coach Xia Xiyao of the Wuhan youth tennis club that this would be the right career move for her. Carlos Menem [show/hide]
Carlos Saúl Menem (born July 2, 1930) is an Argentine politician who was President of Argentina from 1989 to 1999. He is currently an Argentine National Senator for La Rioja Province. Carlos S. Menem was born in 1930 in Anillaco, a small town in the mountainous north of La Rioja Province, Argentina. An Arab Argentine, his parents were born in the town of Yabrud (part of the Ottoman Empire at the time, currently in southwestern Syria, near the border with Lebanon). As a young man, he joined his father as a traveling salesman dealing in feed and sundry items. Menem enrolled in the National University of Córdoba, and received the degree of "Abogado", equivalent to a Bachelor of Laws, in 1955. As a Law student, he became a vocal Peronist, and after President Juan Perón's overthrow that year, he was briefly incarcerated. He later joined the Peronist Party's successor, the Justicialist Party, and was elected President of its La Rioja Province chapter, in 1983. Menem Carlos "el patillas" La Rioja in 1973, a prominent post that left him exposed after the overthrow of President Isabel Martínez de Perón in March 1976. Osama bin Laden [show/hide]
Osama bin Mohammed bin Awad bin Laden ( /oʊˈsɑːmə bɪn moʊˈhɑːmɨd bɪn əˈwɑːd bɪn ˈlɑːdən/; Arabic: أسامة بن محمد بن عوض بن لادن‎, ʾUsāmah bin Muḥammad bin ʿAwaḍ bin Lādin; March 10, 1957 – May 2, 2011), was the founder of Al-Qaeda, the jihadist organization responsible for the September 11 attacks on the United States, along with numerous other mass-casualty attacks against civilian and military targets. He was a member of the wealthy Saudi bin Laden family, and an ethnic Yemeni Kindite. Bin Laden was on the American Federal Bureau of Investigation's (FBI) lists of Ten Most Wanted Fugitives and Most Wanted Terrorists for his involvement in the 1998 U.S. embassy bombings. From 2001 to 2011, bin Laden was a major target of the War on Terror, with a US$25 million bounty by the Federal Bureau of Investigation. On May 2, 2011, bin Laden was shot and killed inside a private residential compound in Abbottabad, Pakistan, by U.S. Navy SEALs and CIA operatives in a covert operation ordered by United States President Barack Obama. al-Qaeda [show/hide]
al-Qaeda ( /ælˈkaɪdə/ al-KY-də; Arabic: القاعدة‎ al-qāʿidah, Arabic: [ælqɑːʕɪdɐ], translation: "The Base" and alternatively spelled al-Qaida and sometimes al-Qa'ida) is a global militant Islamist organization founded by Osama bin Laden at some point between August 1988 and late 1989. It operates as a network comprising both a multinational, stateless army and a radical Sunni Muslim movement calling for global Jihad and a strict interpretation of sharia law. It has been designated as a terrorist organization by the United States, the United Kingdom, various other countries, the United Nations Security Council, the European Union, and NATO (see below). Al-Qaeda has attacked civilian and military targets in various countries. For example, it carried out the September 11 attacks, 1998 US embassy bombings and the 2002 Bali bombings. The US government responded to the September 11 attacks by launching the War on Terror.
